On The Journey, Giants, and Evolution

March 19, 2023

I wrote my first line of code in my second year of high school. Yeah—I started late, I know.

But, I believe I stand, as do all students in UTCS and Turing, "on the shoulders of Giants." 1 These words, while most famously remembered as Newton's diss on Hooke, really resonate with me for a few reasons.

  1. They emphasize the connection between us and the greats: Turing, Hopper, Dijkstra. Throughout my studies, I can only hope to carry this mentality with me; to pursue the ever-breathing knowledge of those who came before. Throughout high school, exploration and a desire for deeper understanding have supplied me with a more nuanced view of computer science as a whole. I've come to see it as more than a mere tool for application, but as a beautiful subject in its own right—centered on the art of efficiency.

  2. Within, there's a warm sense of gratitude. These words convey a sense of respect that is due from all learners; after all, we all gained our ability to access new information from someone. In many cases, it's our professors, our teachers, our mentors—but it could just as easily be the person who wrote the textbook, published their information on that website, or made the YouTube video. We all build upon others' accomplishments, forging progress: a reminder of just how tall we are when we stand together.

I see the strength and interdisciplinary beauty of computer science—a field that pushes knowledge far beyond computing and programming alone. It's possible to create realistic lighting in a video game with knowledge of optical physics, and machine learning functions on the basis of multivariable calculus. In college, I'm excited to explore these connections and use them to pioneer new, innovative solutions.

So, my true journey through computer science starts here 2.

Footnotes

  1. Yes, I know that Newton was likely making fun of Hooke's ostensibly short stature (as my Physics C teacher, Mrs. Watson, once gave a half-hour lecture over). And as a short-ish guy myself, I probably ought to be a little upset by the second-hand burn factor. Somehow though, I can't help but admire the fact that Newton simultaneously roasted a rival while delivering one of the most beautiful and meaningful lines ever written. The man was a genius: as if that wasn't already apparent by his invention of Calculus. ^

  2. Somehow, I find the sentiment quite endearing. "The Eyes of Texas" played on the Carillion is cute—I can't wait to hear it on campus. ^
