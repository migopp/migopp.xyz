"Good" People

June 4, 2023

What does it mean to be a "good" person?

I'm sure many people would argue that certain traits (such as compassion, selflessness, or intelligence) make a person "good," but is it really that simple?

At the basis of the answer, one must confront what goodness means as a concept, and extend it to what it even means to be a person. Is goodness just moral virtuousness—maybe even just the absence of evilness? Or, is it more broad than that?

"Goodness"

Consider the possibility that the idea of "goodness," when applied to "good people," is similar to the idea of "goodness" applied to being a "good cook," "good electrician," or "good runner." Behind all of these descriptions, there is a purpose—chefs cook, so a "good" chef cooks well, producing high quality food that they (or people in general) can enjoy; electricians fix wiring and stuff, so, naturally, a "good" electrician fixes with expertise and efficiency; runners—well—run, so a "good" runner runs well, breaking records, winning races, clocking fast times. This logic continues for most descriptions of "good" things; they do their jobs well.

People

Building off of our new consideration, it would follow that "good people" do the job of being a person well. But what is the job of a person?

A person... lives?

Isn't that a deeply personal question? Then, determining the requirements for being a good person presupposes that we have collectively decided what the purpose of being a person is—but have we? Can we even do that?

Without them, we can't reasonably determine our profieciency in executing this task of being human. So, is this even a valuable metric anyway? Maybe not.

Does everyone have the same purpose? Maybe not—and, maybe, whether or not someone is a "good" person all depends on purpose. Perhaps there are many, and we all lay at the fork of an infinitely diverging path. And, further, consider that our traditional notion of "good" people builds itself upon the idea of being virtuous and moral—coinciding with the purpose of living for others, of collectivism. Is it possible, then, that being a "good" person as a concept is simply a derivative of being "good" at anything?

Toasters and Concessions

All of these ideas are fun to think about, but they break down fairly easily.

For example, consider a hypothetical person who believes it is their life mission and sole purpose to go to random swimming pools and throw toasters into them—electrocuting innocent people. If they were to carry this out well, then, technically, they would be a "good" person by the standards set above. They achieve their purpose as a person well. Who are we to tell them that the belief of their purpose is not their purpose?

I hope it's easy to see that when you view "goodness" this way, it quickly becomes meaniningless.

But, morality is also individualistic; there's no objectivity, only different schools of thought. So, the morality that is inherently implied when we call a person "good" is also dependent on that context. If this toaster-thrower lived in a hypothetical society where this electrocution was considered moral, their actions would constitute that of a "good" person's. Yet again, this devolves because people could, theoretically, pick an arbitrary moral code to justify any action as "good."

I wonder if there is any satisfying answer.
