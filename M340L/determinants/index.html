Determinants

Three Properties of the Determinant

\(\det{I}=1\)

Exchanging any two rows reverses the sign of the determinant.

For any square matrix:

\[ \begin{vmatrix} ta & tb \\ c & d \end{vmatrix} =t \begin{vmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{vmatrix} \]

\[ \begin{vmatrix} a+a' & b+b' \\ c & d \end{vmatrix} = \begin{vmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{vmatrix} + \begin{vmatrix} a' & b' \\ c & d \end{vmatrix} \]

Findings from Determinant Properties

Notice that having two equal rows guarantees that the determinant is \(0\). This can be seen through the second property. Let \(A\) be a \(2 \times 2\) matrix with rows \(a_1=a_2\). Then, exchange \(a_1\) and \(a_2\). The determinant sign must be flipped, but the matrix \(A\) remains the same. Thus, the determinant must have been 0 to begin with.

Performing elimination operations does not change the determinant.

\[ \begin{vmatrix} a & b \\ c-la & d-lb \end{vmatrix} = \begin{vmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{vmatrix} \cancel{ -l \begin{vmatrix} a & b \\ a & b \end{vmatrix} } \]

If \(A\) contains a row of zeros, then \(\det{A}=0\). To prove this, take \(t=0\) in third property.

Let \(U\) be an upper-triangular matrix.

\[ \det{U}= \begin{vmatrix} d_1 & * & \cdots & \cdots & * \\ 0 & d_2 & * &\cdots & * \\ \vdots & 0 & \ddots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & \cdots & \cdots & \cdots & d_n \end{vmatrix} = \prod_{i=1}^{n}d_i \]

This can be seen by first converting \(U\) to RREF:

\[ \begin{vmatrix} d_1 & 0 & \cdots & \cdots & 0 \\ 0 & d_2 & 0 &\cdots & 0 \\ \vdots & 0 & \ddots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & \cdots & \cdots & \cdots & d_n \end{vmatrix} \]

Then by applying the third property:

\[ \prod_{i=1}^{n}d_i \begin{vmatrix} 1 & 0 & \cdots & \cdots & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 &\cdots & 0 \\ \vdots & 0 & \ddots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & \cdots & \cdots & \cdots & 1 \end{vmatrix} = \prod_{i=1}^{n}d_i \]

So, one way to compute the determinant is to perform elimination and perform a standard multiplication. However, note that the sign of the final answer is based on the number of row exchanges done.

Finally, note that \(\det{A}=0 \implies A\) is singular. Let there exist an arbitrary singular \(A\). Then, elimination will produce a \(0\) row. In which case, the determinant will be \(0\).

A final two: \(\det{AB}=(\det{A})(\det{B})\) and \(\det{A^T}=\det{A}\).

On Cofactors and Determinants

For simplicity, first consider $ A= \[\begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix}\]

$.

\[ \begin{align*} \begin{vmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{vmatrix} &= \begin{vmatrix} a & 0 \\ c & d \end{vmatrix} + \begin{vmatrix} 0 & b \\ c & d \end{vmatrix} \\ &= \begin{vmatrix} a & 0 \\ c & 0 \end{vmatrix} + \begin{vmatrix} a & 0 \\ 0 & d \end{vmatrix} + \begin{vmatrix} 0 & b \\ c & 0 \end{vmatrix} + \begin{vmatrix} 0 & b \\ 0 & d \end{vmatrix} \\ &= \begin{vmatrix} a & 0 \\ 0 & d \end{vmatrix} + \begin{vmatrix} 0 & b \\ c & 0 \end{vmatrix} \\ &= ad-bc \end{align*} \]

This general method is called using the cofactors of \(A\). And it generalizes to higher-order matrices.

In the first term of the three-by-three cofactor expansion, for example, notice that for

\[ A=\begin{bmatrix} a & b & c \\ d & e & f \\ g & h & i \end{bmatrix} \]

we get:

\[ a(ei-fh)= a \begin{vmatrix} e & f \\ h & i \end{vmatrix} = \begin{vmatrix} a & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & e & f \\ 0 & h & i \end{vmatrix} \]

As such, it becomes clear that our cofactor expansion is a summation of subdeterminants. These subdeterminants actually are the aforementioned cofactors (for the two-by-two case, that subdeterminant was just trivially computed).

As such, we have the generalized cofactor formula:

\[ \det{A}=a_{11}C_{11} + a_{12}C_{12} + \dots + a_{1n}C_{1n} \]

Where \(C_{ij}\) represents the cofactor (signed subdeterminant) for \(a_{ij}\). Note that this \(C_{ij}\) is technically signed based on the parity of \(i+j\); though, it can be manually computed in each case. The sign is built in for this formula out of simplicity.

Inversion via Determinants

We’ve known (and used) the following formula for two-by-two matrix inversion for a while

\[ \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix}^{-1} = \frac{1}{ad-bc} \begin{bmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{bmatrix} \]

This formula is generalized as:

\[ A^{-1}=\frac{C^T}{\det{A}} \]

Where \(C\) is the cofactor matrix of \(A\)—a matrix where each entry of \(A\) is replaced with its cofactor.

Proving the above formula simply requires proving the following fact:

\[ AC^T=(\det{A})I \]

To show why this is true, expand:

\[ AC^T= \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} & \cdots & a_{1n} \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ a_{n1} & \cdots & a_{nn} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} C_{11} & \cdots & C_{1n} \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ C_{n1} & \cdots & C_{nn} \end{bmatrix} \]

Now, we see that all diagonal entries are \(\det{A}\) as they represent the cofactor formula for the determinant of \(A\). All other entries are \(0\) because they represent taking the determinant of a singular matrix. To illuminate this fact, take a look at the two-by-two case.

$$ A= \[\begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix}\] C= \[\begin{bmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{bmatrix}\]

$$

Multiply the first row of \(A\) by the second column of \(C\) to get:

\[ \begin{bmatrix} a & b \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} -b \\ a \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -ab+ab \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \end{bmatrix} \]

Whose determinant is trivially \(0\).

Geometry of Determinants

Let \(A\) be a three-by-three matrix. Then, geometrically, \(\det{A}\) represents the volume of the box (a parallelepiped, to be precise) whose edges are formed by the row vectors of \(A\).

A few special cases arise. Take \(A=I\). Then, \(\det{A}\) represents the unit cube. Also, take \(A=Q\). Then \(\det{A}\) also represents a unit cube, but rotated through space (though, with a corner still locked at \((0, 0, 0)\)).

This geometric interpretation is analogous in other orders of matrix \(A\), as well. For instance, consider the 2D case. Then, \(\det{A}\) represents the area of the parallelogram found by \(a_1 + a_2\), where \(a_1\) and \(a_2\) are the columns vectors of \(A\).

Often, it is useful to find the area of a triangle in \(\R^2\) that does not start at \((0, 0)\). Here is one such formula:

\[ \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2} \begin{vmatrix} x_1 & y_1 & 1 \\ x_2 & y_2 & 1 \\ x_3 & y_3 & 1 \end{vmatrix} \]
