Projections

Projections onto Vectors

The fundamental projection equation:

\[ a^T(b-xa)=0 \]

Finds the scaling factor \(x\) s.t. \(p=xa\) where \(p\) is the projection from a vector with an orthogonal component, \(b\), onto a one-dimensional subspace \(a\). The derivation continues as follows:

\[ \begin{align*} xa^Ta &= a^Tb \\ x &= \frac{a^Tb}{a^Ta} \\ p=ax &\implies p=\frac{aa^T}{a^Ta}b \end{align*} \]

It can also be simply remembered as (with a shift in perspective):

\[ \textrm{proj}_ab=(\frac{b \cdot a}{a \cdot a})a \]

Projection Matrices

A projection matrix \(P\) acts upon a vector to project a vector onto a subspace. Dictated:

\[ \textrm{proj}_ab=Pb \]

Where \(\textrm{proj}_ab\) symbolizes the projection of the vector \(b\) onto the subspace \(a\) (in this case, \(a\) is just a line).

In the case of a projection onto a vector:

\[ P=\frac{aa^T}{a^Ta} \]

Properties of Projection Matrices

Observe that \(P\) is symmetric.

\[ \frac{1}{a^Ta}(aa^T)^T=\frac{aa^T}{a^Ta} \]

Also, suppose \(P\) is projected onto itself. Then, \(P^2=P\). As such:

\[ P^k={P} \]

Projection onto Planes

Suppose we want to project a vector \(b\) onto a plane \(A\) given its basis \(\{a_1, a_2\}\). We know that the projected vector can be written as \(p=ca_1+da_2\) because the projected vector will be in \(\mathcal{C}(A)\).If we say the scaling vector \(\begin{bmatrix} c \\ d \end{bmatrix}=\hat{x}\), then we have

\[ \begin{align*} A^T(b-A\hat{x})=0 \\ A^TA\hat{x}=A^Tb \end{align*} \]

Observe that the projection matrix is revealed through this last equation:

\[ \begin{align*} \hat{x}=(A^TA)^{-1}A^Tb \\ P=A(A^TA)^{-1}A^T \end{align*} \]

All of the above properties for projection matrices hold for our new formula. Namely:

\[ P=P^T=P^2 \]

\(P^T\):

\[ \begin{align*} P^T&=(A(A^TA)^{-1}A^T)^T \\ &= A^{T^T}(A^TA)^{-1}A^T \tag*{$\because (A^TA)$ is symmetric} \\ &= A(A^TA)^{-1}A^T \end{align*} \]

\(P^2\):

\[ \begin{align*} P^2&=A(A^TA)^{-1}A^TA(A^TA)^{-1}A^T \\ &=A(A^TA)^{-1}\cancel{(A^TA)(A^TA)^{-1}}A^T \\ &=A(A^TA)^{-1}A^T \\ \end{align*} \]

Projection Use Case

It may be the case that \(Ax=b\) has no solution. So, we solve \(A\hat{x}=p\) instead, so \(p\) is the closest approximation.

Least Squares

One application for these projection matrices is least squares determination, which is a method for finding the best-fit line of a set of data.

In this case, we often have a degenerate \(A\) with too many equations, and the system \(Ax=b\) has no solution. So, instead we find the minimized error by projecting onto a best-fit line. This application utilizes the formula derived earlier:

\[ A^TA\hat{x}=A^Tb \]

Solving for \(\hat{x}\) gives the constants forming the best-fit line.
